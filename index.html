Chapter One

The ones who live
beneath the sea

Orcas are matrilineal. They are born into names, into songs,into a mother who will never leave them. Children stay their entire lives. Daughters carry the lineage forward. A pod is not a group — it is a family that never disperses.

The Lummi Nation of the Pacific Northwest call them qwe lhol mechen — the people who live beneath the sea. Not metaphor. Recognition. The Southern Residents have moved through these waters for thousands of years across three pods: J, K, and L.

Chapter Two

The years they
came for the calves

Between 1961 and 1976, the Pacific Northwest became the primary hunting ground for a new industry, capturing wild orcas for display. The younger ones were cheapest to ship, easiest to condition.

1961

Wanda, the first Pacific Northwest orca captured — sold to Marineland of the Pacific. The trade begins.

1965

Shamu captured off Whidbey Island, sold to SeaWorld. A lineage becomes a logo.

1967

5 Southern Residents taken in 17 days. Three drown in the nets.

1970

Penn Cove. 80+ orcas surrounded with speedboats, planes, explosives. Seven taken. At least five killed — carcasses secretly sunk to hide the count.

1972

Marine Mammal Protection Act passed. SeaWorld exploits loopholes and continues capturing calves.

1976

Budd Inlet, Olympia. A state official witnesses the operation, calls the governor. A judge rules against SeaWorld in two weeks. Pacific Northwest captures end.

1976–1989

Captures move to Iceland. 54 more taken. Tilikum and Keiko among them.

Chapter Three

The ocean
becomes a dream

Corky was captured in 1969, age four, from the Northern Residents near Vancouver Island. She has been at SeaWorld San Diego for over fifty years. Her family — the A5 pod — still swims those same waters. Her brother Fife is alive today.

None of Corky's seven pregnancies produced a surviving calf. In the wild, a calf stays with its mother for life. In captivity, calves were routinely separated and transferred between facilities. The acoustic bonds that define orca society cannot be recreated in concrete.

"You could hear the whales screaming to each other, back and forth across the water. I can still hear it sometimes." — Eyewitness to the 1976 Budd Inlet capture

Chapter Four

J35 — Tahlequah

Born 1998. J17 matriline. She has carried grief twice — through waters her ancestors knew before captivity took half her community.

2018

Tahlequah's calf was born July 24th and survived thirty minutes. She carried the body for 17 days across more than 1,000 miles of the Salish Sea — a journey researchers documented as epimeletic behavior: grief given form in motion. Her family followed alongside the entire time.

2024

On December 20, 2024, Tahlequah gave birth to J61 — a female calf. Identified Christmas Eve. Confirmed dead New Year's Eve. Tahlequah was observed carrying the body for at least 11 days. The second time. The same mother. The same ocean. Six years apart.

Grief exists beyond human language. She moved through the same water her family had always moved through — but with something more: the weight of what could not be saved, and the refusal to let go.

Chapter Five

Seventy-four remain.
K pod is fading.

The 2025 census recorded 74 Southern Residents across three pods. The number hovers near this low for years — births consistently failing to outpace deaths, calves dying within weeks of being born.

27 J Pod
14 K Pod
33 L Pod

K pod: lowest count since surveys began. Highest likelihood of extinction within 50 years. There are calls K pod makes that no other group makes — calls that will vanish with them.

Threats today

  • ◌ Salmon depletion — Southern Residents eat almost exclusively Chinook. As salmon runs collapse, mothers cannot nurse calves on insufficient calories. Nutritional stress directly impairs reproduction.
  • ◌ Vessel noise — Boat traffic masks the acoustic signals used to hunt and communicate. In a species whose entire social structure is built on sound, noise is severance — a mother cannot locate her calf.
  • ◌ Toxic contaminants — PCBs concentrate in orca fat and pass through mothers' milk to calves. They suppress immune systems and cause stillbirths.
  • ◌ The demographic legacy of capture — The 48 removed weren't replaceable. Pods carry cultural knowledge: dialects, feeding grounds, remembered routes. What was taken was also accumulated wisdom.

On November 4, 2024, L Pod returned to Penn Cove for the first time in 50 years. They had avoided the site — and taught each subsequent generation to avoid it. L25 "Ocean Sun," the oldest surviving Southern Resident and the only member alive during the 1970 captures, entered the cove that day. The last witness returned to the place of the wound.

Coda

The living carry
the dead forward

Tahlequah's matriline continues. J pod has 27 members. The census is conducted every year — which means people are still counting, still watching, still refusing to look away. The act of witness is itself a form of care.

L25 — Ocean Sun
Estimated born 1928. The oldest Southern Resident.
She was alive when they came for her pod in 1970.
She returned to Penn Cove in November 2024.
She is still swimming.

Grief in a species this social is not private. When Tahlequah swam with her dead calf, her family followed alongside. The matriline holds what the individual cannot. The living carry the dead forward.
